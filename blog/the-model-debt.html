The Model Debt: Why 67% of Enterprises Are Running More LLMs Than They Can Manage — And Paying for Every One

June 27, 2026
AI FinOps Infrastructure Product Management

Your team is running GPT-4o for customer support, Claude Sonnet 4.6 for code generation, Gemini 2.5 for document analysis, and a fine-tuned Llama 4 for internal knowledge retrieval. Every model was evaluated, approved, and deployed by a different team in a different quarter for a different use case. Nobody remembers who owns the GPT-4 eval suite. Nobody wants to touch the Claude prompts because they "just work." And when Sonnet 4.7 drops next month, every single team will evaluate it from scratch without retiring a single model they already have.

I call this model debt. It is the compounding operational cost of maintaining overlapping LLM deployments that nobody ever retires. And it is quietly becoming one of the largest unbudgeted liabilities in enterprise AI.

Datadog's State of AI Engineering 2026 report analyzed telemetry from over 1,000 production customers and found the evidence in the data: older models persist at the same usage levels as newer releases long after they should have been retired. GPT-4o still accounts for 22% of LLM calls months after Claude Sonnet 4.6 hit 17% adoption in its first month. Sonnet 4.5 lingers at 19% — nearly the same share as its successor. Each overlapping model adds operational overhead, evaluation burden, and provider-deprecation risk that shows up on nobody's budget.

The Flexera 2026 AI Pulse Report found that 36% of organizations overspent on AI applications and 14% report outright wasted AI spend. Most of that waste lives in model portfolios that have never been audited. Enterprises accumulate models the same way they accumulate SaaS subscriptions — by adding and never subtracting.

The five costs of model debt

Every model in your portfolio carries five distinct costs. Most teams track only the first one.

1. Inference spend. The obvious one. Per-token cost shows up on the cloud bill. This is the only cost most FinOps teams track, and even then they track it at the provider level, not the model level. When your "AI & ML" line item grows 40% quarter over quarter, you cannot tell which model drove the increase.

2. Evaluation burden. Every model needs its own eval suite. Different tokenizers, different prompt formats, different safety filters, different failure modes. When a team adds Gemini 2.5 Flash to the portfolio, they rebuild evals from scratch. When GPT-5 drops, they rebuild again. The eval suites never consolidate because each model family behaves differently enough that a single eval harness cannot cover all of them. The LangChain State of Agent Engineering 2026 survey found that quality evaluation remains the #1 barrier to production deployment for the second year running, cited by 32% of practitioners. Model debt multiplies this problem by the number of models you run.

3. Prompt fragmentation. Prompts optimized for Claude do not transfer to Gemini. System instructions that work on GPT-4o hallucinate on Llama 4. Your team ends up maintaining N versions of every prompt — one per model. Each version needs separate testing, separate monitoring, and separate maintenance. Over time, prompt drift across model families becomes impossible to track. The prompt that works today breaks tomorrow because someone updated the system instructions for the GPT version and forgot to port the change to the Claude version.

4. Deprecation risk. Every model you run has a deprecation clock. As I covered in the Deprecation Crisis, model versions turn over faster than enterprises can re-deploy. When you run five models from three providers, you effectively have five ticking deprecation bombs. Each one requires re-evaluation, re-grounding, and re-deployment when the vendor sunsets it. The cost of this migration is never budgeted. It is discovered.

5. Compliance drag. Every new model requires a fresh review cycle: data handling assessment, SOC 2 scope update, EU AI Act compliance check, internal security review. Most enterprises that have completed one model review are not eager to repeat it. The result: teams quietly adopt new models without telling legal or security, creating a shadow AI problem that compounds with every unregistered model. The governance team believes the organization runs three models. The engineering team knows it runs seven. Nobody has the full picture.

Why model debt compounds

Model debt behaves like financial debt. A single model is manageable. Two models double your evaluation and prompt surface area. Three models create combinatoric complexity — now every workflow needs testing against every model combination. At five models, the maintenance cost exceeds the value any single model delivers.

The dynamic is uniquely damaging in enterprise AI because of how models are adopted. A data scientist evaluates a new model for a specific use case, proves it outperforms the incumbent by 12% on one metric, and deploys it. The old model stays running because nothing triggered its retirement. Six months later, the team has four models in production, each the "best" for a narrow scenario, and no process for deciding which one to turn off when a new model makes all four obsolete.

The Stanford HAI 2026 AI Index documents the root cause: inference costs dropped 280x from late 2022 to 2026. When tokens are cheap, there is no financial incentive to consolidate models. But the operational costs I described above — eval burden, prompt fragmentation, deprecation risk, compliance drag — do not scale down with token prices. They scale with model count. The Great Cheapening made it easy to add models. It did nothing to make it easy to remove them.

This is the inversion most teams miss: cheap inference makes model accumulation free in the short term and expensive in the long term. The costs that compound are the ones that do not appear on any invoice.

How to measure and retire model debt

1. Build a model registry before you need one. Every model in your organization should be registered in a central catalog. Include the owner, use case, deployment date, eval results, prompt versions, and — critically — an expiration date. The expiration date is the date by which the model must be re-evaluated or retired. If nobody renews it, the model is automatically flagged for deprecation. This is the single highest-leverage investment you can make in containing model debt. Without a registry, you cannot measure what you have, let alone manage it.

2. Create a model retirement protocol. Define the trigger conditions for retiring a model: a newer version of the same family achieves better or equal performance on your eval suite, a newer model from a different family makes the older model redundant, or the model has not been called in 30 days. Each trigger should automatically open a migration ticket with a default timeline. The protocol makes retirement predictable instead of painful.

3. Run a quarterly model portfolio review. Every quarter, review your model registry against usage data, cost data, and new model releases. Identify candidates for retirement. Ask the hard question: "If we turned this model off tomorrow, which workflow would break, and would the team that built it notice?" Model portfolio reviews are the equivalent of a FinOps right-sizing exercise. Most teams find 20-30% of their deployed models are delivering near-zero incremental value.

4. Budget for model migration as an operational expense. Model migration is not a project. It is a recurring operational cost, like cloud infrastructure or employee training. Budget 5-10% of your total AI spend for model migration and retirement. This includes the engineering time to re-eval, the testing cycles to validate, and the monitoring changes to track. When migration is a line item instead of a surprise, the organization stops treating model retirement as an optional cleanup activity and starts treating it as a required maintenance activity.

5. Track model debt as a platform KPI. Add three metrics to your AI platform dashboard: active model count by provider, average model age (time since first deployment), and model-to-use-case ratio (how many models per distinct workflow). These metrics are the AI equivalent of tech debt velocity. When model count grows faster than use case count, you are accumulating debt. When average model age exceeds your deprecation window, you are carrying stranded assets.

The portfolio approach

The teams that manage model debt well think like portfolio managers, not feature owners. They evaluate model additions against portfolio diversity (do we need another provider?), portfolio overlap (does this model cover use cases we already serve?), and portfolio exit cost (can we turn this model off in 30 days if a better one appears?).

The portfolio frame changes the incentive structure. Instead of asking "is this model better on the benchmark?" the portfolio manager asks "does this model improve our portfolio's cost-performance ratio enough to justify the operational overhead of adding it?" Most models fail the second question even when they pass the first.

The counterintuitive insight from the teams I've studied: the best model strategy is almost never the one with the best benchmark scores. It is the one with the smallest portfolio that covers the widest range of use cases. Smaller portfolios mean fewer evals, fewer prompts to maintain, fewer compliance reviews, fewer deprecation risks. They also mean faster iteration — a three-model portfolio can re-evaluate against a new release in days, while a ten-model portfolio takes weeks.

Model debt is the hidden tax on every enterprise AI program that has been running for more than six months. It is not a technology problem. It is a portfolio management problem. And it is solvable with the same tools every investment manager already uses: a ledger of what you own, a discipline for when to sell, and a willingness to admit that adding one more model makes the entire portfolio harder to manage, not easier.
