The Great Cheapening: How 280x Cheaper Inference Rewrote the Economics of AI

June 15, 2026
AI Economics Product Management

The most important number in AI right now isn't a benchmark score. It's $0.07.

That's roughly the cost per million tokens for inference on a frontier model in early 2026. Down from about $20 in late 2022. A 280x drop in under four years. This single curve explains almost everything that's happened since — the explosion in adoption, the agentic turn, even the governance mess we're all dealing with.

When something gets 280x cheaper, the set of things you can do with it changes categorically. Not linearly. Categorically.

From luxury to utility

In late 2022, you thought carefully before every API call. A multi-step agent looping through five reasoning steps before answering? That cost real money before it even returned a result. Agentic systems weren't just technically immature — they were economically irrational.

At today's prices, that same loop costs basically nothing. You can afford to fail, retry, explore multiple paths, and discard bad branches without watching the bill. The economic precondition for agentic AI wasn't better reasoning. It was cheaper tokens.

But here's what keeps me up: the cheapening created the boom. It didn't create the discipline.

The shadow economy

Stanford HAI estimates the value of generative AI tools to US consumers reached $172 billion annually by early 2026. That's roughly Hungary's GDP, flowing as surplus to people using free and low-cost AI tools.

Almost nobody in enterprise talks about this. Every board conversation I've sat in frames AI value through cost reduction or revenue generation. Almost none ask: what value are our employees already capturing with personal accounts, and how do we harness that?

Employees are getting massive value from AI on their own time with their own tools. Enterprise tools go through security review, procurement, integration, training. Consumer tools just work. The gap between the two is widening, not narrowing.

The trap

The 280x drop is a blessing and a trap. Cheap inference makes bad architecture survivable. I've seen teams build agentic loops that call the model five times when a single call with a better prompt would do. The cost is negligible, so nobody optimizes. But the latency, the error surface, the maintenance burden — all of it compounds.

In the old era, you had to be surgical. Every API call had a budget. Now that forcing function is gone, and with it went a lot of good design habits.

Cheap inference gave everyone volume. It didn't give anyone transformation.

What I'm watching next

Inference costs are still falling. We'll probably hit $0.01 per million tokens within a year. At that price, the distinction between "think about calling AI" and "always call AI" disappears entirely.

When that happens, three things shift. Agents become background processes running constantly. The governance gap becomes existential — if AI is always on, the blast radius of ungoverned agents is enormous. And value capture becomes the only differentiator. When inference is free, every company has access to the same capability. The winners will be the ones who can answer "what did this AI spend actually produce?"

The 280x cheapening created the AI industry we have today. But it also created a generation of product decisions shaped by abundance rather than intentionality. The next phase won't be about making AI cheaper. It'll be about making it count.
