The 68-Point Gap: Why 88% of Companies Say They Use AI But Only 20% Actually Do

June 15, 2026
AI Adoption Product Management

I sat through a vendor presentation last year when someone dropped the 88% number — how many companies use AI. Sounded impressive. Then I checked the Census Bureau data and found a different story. They asked one question: "Did your business use AI in the last two weeks?" The answer as of May 2026 was just under 20%.

That's a massive gap between what executives say and what businesses actually do.

I've been on both sides of this. I've run pilots that got called "AI adoption" before the demo finished. I've watched those same pilots collect dust when security, procurement, and integration showed up. The gap isn't about dishonesty — it's about what we're counting. A senior exec answering McKinsey remembers that Copilot trial from Q2. The Census respondent counts the tool their accounting team uses daily. Same word, different reality.

McKinsey asks C-suite folks at big companies. The Census reaches over a million firms of all sizes. Both are right — they're just measuring different things. McKinsey captures intent and experiments. The Census captures operational reality. The gap between them is the distance from "we tried it" to "we depend on it."

I see three reasons for the gap. First, "AI" covers everything from a ChatGPT wrapper to a multi-agent supply chain system — the definition is doing heavy lifting. Second, pilots aren't production. A consumer downloads ChatGPT and that's adoption. An enterprise needs security review, procurement, integration, and governance before anything touches a real workflow. Third, ROI is concentrated — only a minority of organizations report any bottom-line impact from AI. When the CFO doesn't see impact, the tool stays a pilot.

The optimist in me sees massive opportunity. Most of the "AI transformation" narrative is still ahead of us. The pessimist sees a warning — AI spend is priced against the 88% narrative, not the 20% reality. As a product manager, I see a roadmap. Closing the gap takes the boring stuff: workflow redesign, integration, cost controls, governance. The same stuff that separates a demo from a product.

I've stopped asking whether AI works. The answer is almost always yes in a demo. I ask: is it being used operationally, or is it a pilot?
