The 68-Point Gap: Why 88% of Companies Say They Use AI But Only 20% Actually Do

June 15, 2026
AI Adoption Product Management

I sat through a vendor presentation last year when someone dropped the 88% number—how many companies use AI. Sounded impressive. Then I checked the Census Bureau data and found a different story.

They asked one question: "Did your business use AI in the last two weeks?" The answer as of May 2026? Just 19.8%.

That's a 68-point gap between what executives say and what businesses actually do. It's the most important AI metric I've seen all year.

I've been on both sides of this. I've run pilots that got called "AI adoption" before the demo finished. I've watched those same pilots collect dust when security, procurement, and integration showed up. The gap isn't about dishonesty. It's about what we're counting.

A senior exec answering McKinsey remembers that Copilot trial from Q2. The Census respondent counts the tool their accounting team uses daily. Same word. Different reality.

Two surveys, two realities

McKinsey asks C-suite folks at big companies. The Census reaches 1.2 million firms of all sizes. Both are right. They're just measuring different things. McKinsey captures intent and experiments. The Census captures operational reality.

The gap between them is the distance from "we tried it" to "we depend on it."

Among firms with 250+ employees, AI adoption hits about 37%. Even the most AI-active segment of the economy is barely a third operational. That matters when Gartner projects $2.59 trillion in global AI spending this year. If 80% of that funds projects that never go live, that's not investment. It's tuition.

Why the gap exists

I see three reasons:

1. The definition is doing heavy lifting. In 2026, "AI" covers everything from a ChatGPT wrapper to a multi-agent supply chain system. When someone asks "are you using AI?" the socially desirable answer is yes. The honest answer is usually "sort of."

2. Pilots aren't production. A consumer downloads ChatGPT and counts as adopted. An enterprise needs security review, procurement, integration, training, and governance before a tool touches a real workflow. The pilot graveyard is well-populated.

3. ROI is concentrated. Only 39% of organizations report any EBIT impact from AI. Most of the consumer surplus goes to individuals, not P&L statements. When the CFO doesn't see impact, the tool stays a pilot.

What the gap means

The optimist in me sees a massive opportunity. Most of the "AI transformation" narrative is still ahead of us. The gap is the surface area for real deployment.

The pessimist sees a warning. The AI spend is priced against the 88% narrative. If markets start measuring against the Census number instead of the McKinsey number, the repricing will hurt.

As a product manager, I see a roadmap. The gap is the distance between experimentation and operational value. Closing it takes the boring stuff: workflow redesign, integration contracts, cost controls, governance. The same stuff that separates a demo from a product.

The question I'm watching

I've stopped asking whether AI "works." The answer is almost always yes in a demo. I ask: is it being used operationally, or is it a pilot? What would need to change for it to cross from one column to the other?

The 88% number will keep making headlines. The 20% number is the one I'm watching. That's where the real work lives.
