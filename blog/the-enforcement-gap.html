The Enforcement Gap: Why 82% of Execs Trust Their AI Policies But Only 14% Can Enforce Them

June 16, 2026
AI Governance Security Product Management

I've been talking to a lot of teams about AI governance lately. The conversation usually starts with "yes, we have a policy" and ends with "...but we don't really enforce it."

Turns out, that's most organizations. Something like 82% of executives are confident their policies protect against unauthorized agent actions. But way fewer have actual enforcement at runtime. That's a massive gap between confidence and reality.

And it's worse than it looks. Most companies enforce AI security through written policies and the honor system. Another big chunk relies on post-event scanning — catching violations after the damage is done. We're building at production speed on a governance foundation that barely exists.

The three numbers that bother me

High confidence, low enforcement. Executives believe their policies work, but the technical controls to enforce them simply aren't deployed. Meanwhile, most enterprises have unknown AI agents running in their environments. You can't enforce policy against agents you don't know exist.

Lots of incidents, almost no visibility. Most organizations have confirmed or suspected AI agent security incidents. Yet only a fraction have runtime visibility into what their agents are actually doing. Most are discovering violations through post-mortems rather than prevention.

Scope violations everywhere, almost no automatic blocks. Over half of organizations have had AI agents exceed their intended permissions. When scope violations occur, almost nobody automatically blocks the action. They rely on human review — after the agent has already acted.

The pattern is clear. We have dashboards. We do not have guardrails.

Why enforcement is harder than writing a policy

Writing an AI governance policy is an exercise in words. Enforcing it is an exercise in architecture. That difference is where the risk lives.

A policy says "agents must not exceed their approved scope." Enforcement requires answering: what is scope? How do you measure it at runtime? What happens when it's breached — log, warn, or halt? Every layer of the stack — identity, authorization, observability — must be wired together in real time. Most enterprises haven't done this wiring because the pressure to ship outpaced the infrastructure to govern.

This is the hidden reason so many AI pilots fail. Not because the policy doesn't exist. Because when security asks "how do we actually enforce this?" the answer is "we'll figure that out in production." And production never comes.

What real enforcement looks like

The minority that actually enforces governance at runtime shares three patterns:

Pre-execution controls. Every model call, tool invocation, and side effect is checked against policy before it executes. If the agent lacks authorization, it gets a DENY before it acts. Not a log entry after.

Agent identity. Every agent gets a unique, scoped identity — just like a human engineer. Every action ties back to that identity. No shared API keys. No standing credentials.

Automatic containment. When scope violations happen — and they will — they don't just log it. They halt the agent, revoke its session, or throttle it to read-only. The rest are one bad agent loop away from an incident that kills the whole program.

The common thread: these organizations treat enforcement as infrastructure, not policy. They build it into the runtime layer so governance isn't something humans remember to do. It's automatic.

A policy you can't enforce is not a policy. It's a hope.
