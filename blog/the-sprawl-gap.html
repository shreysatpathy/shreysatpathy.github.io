The Sprawl Gap: Why 94% of Enterprises Fear AI Agent Proliferation — And Only 13% Are Ready for 150,000 Agents

June 16, 2026
AI Governance Infrastructure Product Management

I've been asking around about how many AI agents companies actually have running. The answer is always the same: "We're not really sure." Engineering built a few. Marketing spun up some with their own budget. Customer success deployed a vendor agent nobody vetted. A couple of teams are running agents on personal accounts. Nobody knows the real number. Nobody knows what they all do. And next quarter, someone is going to ask for a security audit.

This is agent sprawl. And it's the natural consequence of making it cheap and easy to build agents without making it easy to manage them.

Gartner predicts the average Fortune 500 company will go from roughly 15 agents in 2025 to over 150,000 by 2028. That's a 10,000x increase in three years. Only 13% of organizations think they have the right governance in place. The math doesn't add up.

Agents aren't software packages or cloud resources. They're semi-autonomous entities that create, modify, and destroy digital assets. They spawn subagents. They call other agents. They accumulate state and permissions that persist beyond any single workflow. You cannot inventory them with a spreadsheet.

The sprawl arc

Agent sprawl follows a predictable pattern I've seen play out everywhere. It starts with shadow agents — individual teams building agents on personal API keys because it's so easy. Then agent inflation: leadership notices the value, budgets get approved, and every team builds their own using different frameworks and providers. Nobody standardizes anything. Then cost invisibility: with dozens of agents running across uncoordinated stacks, nobody can tell which agent caused the $14,000 spike in this month's invoice. Finally, a governance crisis: the CIO demands an inventory, nobody can produce one, and a blanket freeze goes into effect that just drives agents further underground.

Gartner published a sensible six-step framework for this — establish governance, build a centralized inventory, implement controls, enforce lifecycle management, monitor continuously, assign ownership. But only 13% of organizations have started implementing any of it. The rest are stuck in the spiral.

Sprawl isn't a separate problem. It's what happens when you solve every other barrier to agent adoption — cheaper inference, better models, more platforms — without solving the management problem. You can build the airport, but you still need air traffic control.
