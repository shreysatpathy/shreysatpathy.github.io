The Sprawl Gap: Why 94% of Enterprises Fear AI Agent Proliferation — And Only 13% Are Ready for 150,000 Agents

June 16, 2026
AI Governance Infrastructure Product Management

Your organization has 47 AI agents in production. At least, that's what you think. The engineering team built 12. Marketing spun up 6 more with their own budget. Customer success deployed a vendor agent nobody vetted. Three teams are running agents on their personal OpenAI accounts. Nobody knows how many there actually are. Nobody knows what they all do. And next quarter, someone is going to ask for a security audit.

This is the sprawl gap — and it is the natural consequence of every other gap I've traced in this series. When inference costs collapsed (The Great Cheapening), building agents became cheap. When governance improved (The Governance Multiplier), shipping them became safer. When platforms matured (The Runtime Gap), running them became feasible. The result? A Cambrian explosion of agents that nobody is managing.

OutSystems' 2026 State of AI Development report found that 96% of organizations are already using AI agents in some capacity, and 94% report concern that AI sprawl is increasing complexity, technical debt, and security risk. Gartner's April 2026 research is even more pointed: by 2028, an average global Fortune 500 enterprise will have over 150,000 agents in use, up from fewer than 15 in 2025. That is a 10,000x increase in three years. And only 13% of organizations think they have the right AI agent governance in place.

These numbers describe a structural problem that traditional IT asset management was never designed to solve. Agents are not software packages. They are not cloud resources. They are semi-autonomous entities that create, modify, and destroy digital assets. They spawn subagents. They call other agents. They accumulate state, permissions, and data footprints that persist beyond any single workflow. You cannot inventory them with a spreadsheet, and you cannot govern them with a policy document. The Enforcement Gap — 82% trust policies but only 14% enforce them — becomes the sprawl gap at scale.

The four phases of agent sprawl

Across the enterprise deployment patterns I've studied, agent sprawl follows a predictable progression. Every organization I've seen passes through these phases. The question is whether you reach phase four by design or by surprise.

Phase 1: Shadow Agents. Individual teams discover that building agents is cheap and easy. A data analyst builds an agent to automate their monthly reporting. A sales ops manager deploys an agent to score leads. A customer success lead hooks up an agent to triage support tickets. None of these are approved, documented, or secured. They run on personal API keys. They access data through the employee's credentials — exactly the Identity Gap problem: 80% of agents run on borrowed credentials. This phase typically lasts 3-6 months and produces 10-50 undocumented agents. The SaaS Ultra 2026 analysis found that 39% of enterprises already use more than 10 AI agents — and most are in this phase.

Phase 2: Agent Inflation. The first wave of shadow agents proves value. Leadership notices. Budgets are approved. Now every team wants agents. The problem is that nobody has standardized how agents are built, deployed, or monitored. Each team chooses its own framework (LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen), its own model provider, its own hosting approach. The result is a heterogeneous fleet of agents that cannot be managed from a single control plane. Agent-to-agent communication becomes impossible because they speak different protocols. HUMAN Security's 2026 report documented 7,851% year-over-year growth in agentic AI traffic — most of it ungoverned, untraced, and unaccountable. The Coordination Gap — 57% deploy multi-agent systems but only 12% coordinate them — is phase 2's defining failure mode.

Phase 3: Cost Invisibility. With dozens or hundreds of agents running across uncoordinated stacks, cost attribution becomes impossible. Which agent consumed 50,000 tokens last week? Which team's agent is responsible for the $14,000 spike in the monthly invoice? Nobody can answer these questions because the instrumentation doesn't exist. The TechTarget 2026 FinOps analysis found that a single agentic workflow can consume 5-10x more tokens than a simple chatbot interaction, and most organizations have no per-agent cost tracking. The Observability Blindspot — 80% can't debug their agents — compounds into a cost blindspot that nobody even realizes exists until the CFO demands a breakdown. Gartner projects $2.59 trillion in global AI spending in 2026. Without per-agent cost visibility, most of that spend is unallocated — an asset that cannot be attributed to any business unit or outcome.

Phase 4: Governance Crisis. The CIO demands an agent inventory. Nobody can produce one. A security audit reveals agents accessing sensitive data through unapproved channels. A compliance review shows agents making decisions that should have required human approval. The response is typically a blanket freeze — no new agents until the mess is sorted. But the freeze doesn't stop the existing agents from running, and it doesn't force the shadow agents to surface. It just drives them deeper underground. This is where the sprawl gap becomes an existential risk. Gartner's six-step framework for managing agent sprawl — establish governance, build centralized inventory, implement adaptive controls, enforce lifecycle management, monitor continuously, and assign ownership — is designed to prevent phase 4. But only 13% of organizations have started implementing any of it.

The six-step framework nobody's following

Gartner published its six steps to manage AI agent sprawl in April 2026. The framework is sensible. The adoption is not. Here is what the 13% who are doing this look like — and what the 87% who aren't are missing.

1. Establish agent governance and policies. Set clear rules for when and how agents are built, who can create and share them, and what connectors are permitted. The 13% have a published agent development policy that covers data access scope, model approval requirements, and human-in-the-loop thresholds. The 87% have a "be responsible with AI" paragraph in the employee handbook. The difference is the difference between enforceable policy and aspirational suggestion — the same 68-point gap I traced in The Enforcement Gap.

2. Build a centralized agent inventory. Use AI trust, risk, and security management (AI TRiSM) tools to discover and categorize agents across applications — both sanctioned and shadow. The 13% have an automated discovery mechanism that scans API traffic, GitHub repositories, and cloud accounts for agent signatures. The 87% are conducting manual surveys asking teams to "raise your hand if you've deployed an agent." The SaaS Ultra data shows that 88% of agent pilots never reach production due to governance failures. You cannot govern what you cannot see, and you cannot see what you haven't discovered.

3. Implement adaptive controls. Once you have an inventory, enforce policies based on risk levels. Low-risk internal agents get automated approval. High-risk customer-facing agents require security review. The 13% have a policy engine that evaluates each agent against data sensitivity, decision authority, and blast radius before deployment. The 87% are treating all agents equally — which means they are either over-blocking innovation or under-blocking risk, usually both at the same time.

4. Enforce lifecycle management. Agents have a lifecycle. They are created, deployed, updated, and eventually retired. Most organizations have no mechanism for any of these stages. An agent built for a Q1 campaign is still running in Q3, consuming tokens and accessing data it no longer needs. The surface area for the Injection Gap — 85% of agents execute stranger instructions — grows with every zombie agent that nobody remembers deploying. The 13% have automated retirement policies: agents not used in 90 days are quarantined. The 87% have agents that have been running continuously since 2024.

5. Monitor continuously. This is the evaluability problem all over again. The Evaluation Gap — 64% can't tell if their agents are working — is the sprawl gap at the monitoring layer. You cannot monitor agents you haven't cataloged. The 13% have a unified observability plane that traces every agent's decisions, token consumption, and data access patterns. The 87% are monitoring individual agents in isolation — if they're monitoring them at all. The Arthur AI 2026 playbook recommends OpenTelemetry-based instrumentation as the foundation for agent observability. The 87% haven't heard of OpenTelemetry.

6. Assign ownership. Every agent needs an owner — a human who is responsible for its behavior, its costs, and its compliance. The Mayfield 2026 CXO Survey found that 56% of enterprises now name a dedicated "AI agent owner" or "agentic ops" lead. This is progress. But naming an owner for 150,000 agents — the Gartner 2028 projection — is not a people problem. It is an automation problem. You need fleet-level management tooling that treats agents as a distributed workforce, not a collection of scripts.

Sprawl is the sum of every other gap

Every gap I've traced in this series feeds into sprawl. The 88% Problem: pilots that never reach production create a shadow inventory of half-finished agents. The Identity Gap: agents running on borrowed credentials are invisible to IAM systems. The Observability Blindspot: you cannot monitor what you cannot see. The Runtime Gap: infrastructure that cannot support agents in production creates incentives for teams to build their own. The Coordination Gap: uncoordinated agents become uncontrolled agents. The Enforcement Gap: policies without runtime enforcement are theater.

Sprawl is not a separate problem. It is what happens when you solve every other barrier to agent adoption — cheaper inference, better models, more platforms — without solving the management problem. The inference cost collapse made agent creation free. The platform maturation made agent operation feasible. But nothing made agent governance automatic. The 150,000-agent enterprise is coming. The only question is whether it will be governed or chaotic.

The 280x cost collapse made this possible. The 12x shipping multiplier made it desirable. The sprawl gap is what happens when you build the airport but forget the air traffic control.
