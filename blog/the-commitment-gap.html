The Commitment Gap: Why 40% of Enterprise AI Projects Will Be Canceled by 2027 — And Why the Divergence Between Committed and Experimental AI Strategies Is the Most Expensive Decision Nobody Made

July 16, 2026
AI Economics Strategy Product Management

JPMorgan Chase set a $19.8 billion technology budget for 2026 with 2,000 staff dedicated to AI. Their AI program is already self-funding through roughly $2 billion in operational savings and a 10–11% productivity gain across engineering, operations, and fraud detection. SoundHound's June 2028 survey of customer service leaders found that 96% of organizations with active agentic AI deployments report results that met or exceeded ROI expectations. By every headline metric, enterprise AI has never been healthier.

But there's a second story in the data that nobody is leading with. Gartner's 2026 Hype Cycle for Agentic AI carries a warning most conference keynotes skip: more than 40% of agentic AI projects are expected to be canceled by the end of 2027, citing escalating cost, expanded risk surface, and governance that nobody built in advance. The 88% Problem showed that most pilots never reach production. The Commitment Gap is what happens next: the projects that do ship either get serious investment or get starved. There is no middle path.

The same Gartner report that predicts the cancellation wave also forecasts that 40% of enterprise applications will embed task-specific agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% a year earlier. Both predictions are true simultaneously. The market is bifurcating. Companies that approach AI as core infrastructure — like JPMorgan's $19.8B or Databricks processing 1 quadrillion tokens per year from agent workloads — are pulling away. Companies running a dozen underfunded pilot projects with no dedicated team will hit the cancellation wall.

The bifurcation, in data

Deloitte's 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise report surveyed 3,235 leaders and found that worker access to AI rose 50% in 2025. Twice as many leaders as last year report transformative impact from AI. But the same report flags that only 34% of organizations are truly reimagining their businesses — creating new products, reinventing core processes. Another third is redesigning key processes. The final third is using AI at surface level with little to no change. These three groups are not on the same trajectory.

CGI's June 2026 research across 1,800 executives reinforces the pattern: 62% of organizations are applying AI to core business and operational processes, but only 20% extend their AI strategy across their broader ecosystem. The 42-point gap between "applying AI in one function" and "scaling AI across the enterprise" is the Commitment Gap in action. It's not that the 42% in the middle are failing. It's that they're not committing, and in an environment where the committed players are investing at JPMorgan scale, the middle is a dangerous place to be.

The market is starting to price this divergence. Enterprise AI now makes up more than 40% of OpenAI's revenue, on track for parity with consumer by year-end. Hyperscalers are racing to supply the plumbing: Google Cloud introduced an Agentic Data Cloud with cross-cloud Knowledge Catalog, Databricks launched Genie One with general AI agents for business teams, and AWS announced Bedrock Managed Knowledge Base. Each platform release assumes a level of commitment that the average enterprise pilot simply doesn't have.

Why the middle is the most dangerous place

The Commitment Gap isn't about budget size. It's about organizational posture. The teams that commit — and I've now seen this pattern across a dozen enterprises — share three characteristics that the experimental teams lack:

1. Dedicated headcount with executive cover. JPMorgan didn't assign AI as a side project to existing engineering teams. They carved out 2,000 dedicated staff. The Deloitte data confirms that insufficient worker skills are the biggest barrier to integrating AI into workflows. You can't build AI fluency with 20% time. The Fluency Gap showed that 79% of organizations have the budget but not the literacy. Commitment means closing the literacy gap with dedicated, not borrowed, talent.

2. Infrastructure spending that matches ambition. The Scaffolding Gap showed that infrastructure around AI agents costs 8x more than the models. The committed teams budget for this. The experimental teams don't, and their agents fail not because the model isn't good enough but because there's no eval framework, no guardrail system, and no observability pipeline. Gartner flags escalating cost and expanded risk surface as cancellation drivers — both are infrastructure problems, not model problems.

3. Governance built before deployment, not after. The Governance Multiplier showed that companies with AI governance ship 12x more projects to production. Deloitte found that only one in five companies has a mature model for governance of autonomous AI agents. CGI found that only 14% of organizations have enforcement at runtime. The committed teams build guardrails first. The experimental teams treat governance as a post-hoc compliance exercise, which means every agent deployment carries unmanaged risk that eventually becomes a cancellation trigger.

The Jevons Paradox of commitment

The Jevons Trap showed that 67% cheaper tokens produce 320% higher bills because cheaper AI drives more demand. The Commitment Gap adds a second-order effect: committed teams get disproportionate returns from the same cost reduction because they have the infrastructure, talent, and governance to absorb more usage productively. The same 67% price drop that lets an experimental team run a few more pilot iterations lets JPMorgan deploy AI across another line of business. The gap widens with every price reduction.

This is what makes the Commitment Gap structural rather than temporary. AI costs are still falling. Model capabilities are still improving. But the marginal value of each improvement accrues disproportionately to teams that have already committed — because they have the organizational capacity to turn capability into workflow change. The Absorption Gap showed that enterprise AI adoption doubled while organizational capacity stayed flat. Commitment is how you build capacity. Everything else is just running in place.

The most expensive strategy decision nobody debated

The Commitment Gap isn't a technology problem. It's a portfolio strategy problem. Most enterprises are running AI as a venture portfolio: a dozen small bets, each underfunded, each with a six-month horizon, each reporting to a different executive. The logic is diversification. The reality is that none of the bets have enough resources to reach escape velocity, and the organization learns nothing from any of them because the investment never crossed the threshold where real learning begins.

The committed teams run a different portfolio: fewer bets, each one fully resourced, each with a two-year horizon and a dedicated team and an executive sponsor who can unblock cross-functional dependencies. They fail less often not because they're smarter but because their bets have enough oxygen to survive the inevitable setbacks that kill underfunded pilots.

The uncomfortable implication is that the 40% cancellation rate Gartner predicts is not evenly distributed. It will be concentrated among organizations that are neither fully committed nor fully absent — the ones that have enough AI spend to attract board attention but not enough organizational structure to show for it. The committed players will see their cancellation rate closer to 10-15%. The absent players will have nothing to cancel. The middle 40% will bear almost all of the write-offs.

If your organization has ten active AI initiatives, ask yourself: how many have a dedicated team, a two-year budget, an executive sponsor, and a governance model? If the answer is fewer than three, you're in the cancellation zone. Not because the technology doesn't work, but because the commitment gap is the most expensive decision nobody at your table has explicitly debated.
