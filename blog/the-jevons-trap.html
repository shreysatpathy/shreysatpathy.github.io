The Jevons Trap: Why 67% Cheaper Tokens Are Producing 320% Higher AI Bills — And Why the Price Elasticity of AI Demand Is the Most Expensive Economic Force Nobody Budgeted For

July 3, 2026
AI Economics FinOps Product Management

In April 2026, Uber's engineering team hit a wall. The company had allocated its 2026 AI coding budget expecting usage patterns from the previous year. By April — four months into the fiscal year — the budget was gone. Every dollar allocated for AI-assisted development through December had already been consumed. The response from leadership was not to question whether the AI was delivering value. The response was to cut access. Overnight, usage caps went into effect. Developer productivity tools that had been ubiquitous became rationed.

Uber is not alone. Microsoft revoked its developers' Claude Code licenses months after enabling them — not because Claude Code stopped being useful, but because consumption was growing faster than any budget model had anticipated. A Priceline engineering leader told TechCrunch that a routine Cursor contract renewal came back 4-5x more expensive. GitHub abandoned flat-rate Copilot subscriptions in favor of a usage-based "AI Credits" model. Anthropic shifted enterprise pricing from seat-based to consumption-based. Across the industry, the pricing model for AI is restructuring in real time — and most organizations are discovering their budgets were built on assumptions that no longer apply.

This is the Jevons Trap. It is the most expensive economic force in enterprise AI, and almost nobody budgeted for it.

What the Jevons Paradox looks like in AI infrastructure

The Jevons Paradox — named after 19th-century economist William Stanley Jevons, who observed that more efficient coal engines didn't reduce coal consumption but increased it — states that as the cost of using a resource falls, consumption rises by enough to increase total spending. For 150 years, this paradox has held across energy, transportation, and computing. In 2026, it is holding across enterprise AI with a precision that is almost textbook.

The numbers are stark. Blended AI inference costs fell roughly 67% year over year between Q1 2025 and Q1 2026, dropping from approximately $18.40 to $6.07 per million tokens, per Fortune and industry cost analyses. Per-token API costs have fallen roughly 80% year over year, per Stravoris Research. The total cost to achieve a fixed level of capability is falling 9x to 900x per year, depending on the benchmark, per Epoch AI. Inference costs have dropped approximately 280x since late 2022 — the fastest sustained price decline of any technology product in recorded history.

And yet enterprise AI bills are rising faster than ever. Ramp's analysis of real business spending data reveals that token usage among companies tracking AI spend grew 1,001% from January 2025 to April 2026. Dollar spend grew 497% in the same period. The spread between usage growth (10x) and spend growth (5x) is the per-token price decline at work — but total spend still quintupled. Across Ramp's customer base, the median monthly AI cost hit $2,246 in April 2026. The average hit $140,842. That gap — median versus average — tells you that most companies are in early adoption, but a subset is already experiencing Jevons-level cost multiplication. At the 99th percentile, monthly AI spend reached $831,338. In a single month.

Goldman Sachs Research projects that agentic AI will drive a 24-fold increase in token consumption by 2030. Consumer-side consumption alone is expected to multiply 12x. The combination of consumer and enterprise agentic workloads yields 120 quadrillion tokens per month by 2030. Given that per-token costs are falling 60-70% annually, the math of the Jevons Paradox is unambiguous: total AI spend will continue rising even as unit costs collapse. The only question is how much.

The great cheapening showed that 280x cheaper inference rewrote the economics of AI from the supply side. The Jevons Trap is the demand-side mirror: cheaper supply doesn't reduce your bill. It increases the volume of what you consume, and if you haven't modeled the elasticity of your own demand, your budget is already wrong.

Three mechanisms driving the trap

1. Deployment elasticity is near-infinite. When cloud compute got cheaper in the 2010s, organizations deployed more servers. But server count is bounded by architecture — you can only scale a monolith so far before you hit orchestration complexity limits. AI token consumption has no equivalent natural ceiling. Every new agentic workflow, every RAG pipeline, every fine-tuning run, every multi-model evaluation — each is a new token sink. There is no architectural governor on AI consumption the way there was on compute consumption. The median business now uses 9 distinct models. The average uses 16.5. Among businesses running 26+ models, median monthly AI spend is $26,562. Complexity is not just an engineering cost. It is a financial multiplication factor.

2. Agentic workflows are structurally consumption-intensive. A single agentic task can trigger 10-20 LLM calls. The loop tax documented the specific mechanism: agents in endless iteration burn 15x more tokens than standard chat, and failed sessions waste 3.4x more than successful ones. The latency-quality-cost triangle means teams often optimize for quality first, latency second, and cost last. Deloitte's 2026 Tech Trends report identifies that the shift from proof-of-concept to enterprise deployment frequently produces "monthly bills in the tens of millions of dollars" — not because per-token prices rose, but because the volume of inference required to run production agentic workloads at scale is an order of magnitude beyond what anyone modeled during the pilot phase.

3. The pricing model is shifting from fixed to variable in real time. When GitHub moved Copilot to usage-based pricing on June 1, 2026, it signaled the end of the all-you-can-eat era. Microsoft is moving its broader Copilot suite toward the same credit-based structure. Anthropic's enterprise pricing is now consumption-based. The age of flat-rate AI subscriptions — where you could budget a predictable line item — is receding. Every major vendor is moving toward variable pricing tied to token consumption, which means every enterprise's AI bill is becoming a function of how many agents it deploys, how many workflows it runs, and how many loops its agents get stuck in. The move from fixed to variable pricing transfers all the consumption risk from the vendor to the buyer — and most buyers are not ready to manage that risk.

The 73% problem

Fortune reports that 73% of enterprises say their 2026 AI costs exceeded original projections. That 73% is not a budgeting failure. It is a forecasting failure rooted in a misunderstanding of the demand elasticity of AI consumption.

Consider the budget logic most organizations followed in late 2025: "Inference costs dropped 67%. We plan to increase AI usage by roughly 50%. Our net budget impact should be roughly flat or slightly down." This logic is wrong because it assumes usage is independent of price. In reality, cheaper AI doesn't just make existing use cases cheaper. It unlocks entirely new use cases that were previously uneconomical. A 67% price decline doesn't produce a 67% cost reduction. It produces a usage explosion that overwhelms the price decline with volume growth.

The FinOps Foundation's 2026 State of FinOps Report — surveying 1,192 respondents representing $83 billion in annual cloud spend — found that 98% of FinOps practices now track AI spend. But tracking is not forecasting. The cost gap already showed that only 9% of organizations can break down what their AI spend actually bought. The Jevons Trap is that same gap from the other direction: you are spending more because everything is cheaper, and you have no model for how much more you will spend when the price drops again next year.

What the survivors do differently

A small set of organizations is navigating the Jevons Trap without budget blowouts. The patterns are consistent.

1. They model demand elasticity before they deploy. The teams that budget accurately do not assume a linear relationship between price and consumption. They explicitly model that a 50% price decline could produce 2-3x usage growth — and they stress-test their budgets at 5x, 10x, and 20x consumption scenarios. Most budget models include a growth assumption. Almost none include an elasticity coefficient. The difference between a budget that survives contact with Jevons and one that doesn't is whether you assumed your demand was price-sensitive or price-independent.

2. They instrument per-workflow token cost before scaling. The winning teams know the token cost of every workflow — per-task completed, per customer served, per document processed — before they deploy it at scale. They measure the unit economics of AI consumption the way SaaS companies measure customer acquisition cost. Ramp's data shows that per-employee-per-month AI costs range from $5 (light usage) to $442 (heavy usage, 26+ models). The teams that survive Jevons know where they fall on that curve and why. The teams that don't discover their position when the CFO asks why a pilot that cost $200 in testing is costing $12,000 in production.

3. They treat model selection as a procurement function, not an engineering preference. The median enterprise uses 9 models. The range between the cheapest and most expensive frontier model spans approximately 20x: GPT-5-nano at $0.07 per million tokens against GPT-5.5 at $1.42. The winning teams have formal processes for routing queries to the appropriate model tier — not always the most capable one. They apply the principle of cost-proportional capability: don't pay frontier-model prices for classification tasks that a small model can handle with 99% accuracy. A single decision to route all queries through a premium model adds approximately 55.9% cost share for only 45.8% of tokens consumed, per Ramp's April 2026 data. The premium-model premium is real, and it compounds with every new workflow.

4. They set token budgets and guardrails before deployment. This is the operational twin of the loop tax prescription. Every agent should have a maximum token budget per task, per session, and per day. When the agent exceeds the budget, the task should escalate to a human or fall back to a cheaper model. This is standard practice in cloud cost management. In AI, it is still rare. The organizations that treat AI budget enforcement with the same rigor as cloud budget enforcement are the ones whose CFOs are not asking uncomfortable questions in quarterly reviews.

5. They evaluate on-premise inference for steady-state workloads. Lenovo's 2026 TCO analysis demonstrates an 8x cost advantage per million tokens for self-hosted 70B models versus on-demand cloud inference ($0.11 vs. $0.89), scaling to an 18x advantage against frontier API pricing. Deloitte identifies a breakeven threshold at 60-70% of equivalent hardware acquisition costs. For high-volume, predictable inference workloads — the kind that will dominate as agents move from pilots to production — on-premise or dedicated infrastructure economics start to dominate within months, not years. The organizations that survive the Jevons Trap will be the ones that diversify their inference supply chain before the hyperscaler bill forces the conversation.

The trap resets every pricing cycle

The most dangerous feature of the Jevons Trap is that it doesn't resolve. Each new generation of cheaper inference unlocks a new wave of use cases, which drives a new wave of consumption, which produces a new budget overrun. This is not a one-time shock. It is a structural feature of the AI market. Goldman Sachs projects unit costs falling 60-70% per year for the foreseeable future. At that rate of price decline, the only way to keep total spend flat is to cap consumption growth at roughly 60-70% per year — which means deliberately not deploying AI in use cases where the ROI is positive but the volume is large.

Most organizations are not prepared to make that trade-off. They will deploy AI wherever the marginal ROI exceeds the marginal cost — and the marginal cost is dropping faster than any budget cycle can track. The result is a structural pressure on AI budgets that will intensify, not ease, as inference gets cheaper. The value chasm documented that 90% of companies are doubling AI budgets while returns stay flat. The Jevons Trap is the mechanism that produces that chasm.

The organizations that close the Jevons Trap will not be the ones that cut AI spending. They will be the ones that model demand elasticity, instrument unit economics, enforce token budgets, diversify inference supply, and — most importantly — accept that cheaper AI does not mean cheaper AI bills. It means more AI. And more AI costs more, even when each unit costs less.

Everything else is a budget that looked reasonable in January and stopped making sense in April.
