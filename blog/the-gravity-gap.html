The Gravity Gap: Why 83% of Enterprises Are Reversing the Public Cloud AI Migration — And Why Every Public-First Inference Architecture Is the Most Expensive Infrastructure Debt Nobody Repatriated For

June 21, 2026
AI Infrastructure Economics Product Management

Your AI inference pipeline runs on a public cloud GPU cluster. It processes 2 million requests per day at a predictable $0.003 per inference. The architecture is clean, the dashboard is green, and the VP of Engineering is happy. Then the quarterly review reveals the hidden costs: $47,000 in data egress fees, $12,000 in cross-region bandwidth as training and inference data shuttle between clouds, and $8,500 per month in NAT gateway charges because your VPC needs to reach the model API. The per-inference cost that looked like $0.003 was actually $0.041 — and nobody budgeted for the 13x multiplier between the GPU sticker price and the total infrastructure bill.

This is the gravity gap: the widening divergence between the cost of running AI inference where the GPUs are cheapest and the cost of running it where the data, compliance, and latency requirements actually demand it. The Broadcom 2026 State of Private Cloud Survey, drawing on 1,800 IT leaders across Global 2000 organizations, found that production AI inference has flipped from 56% public cloud to 41% in a single year, with private cloud now hosting 56% of enterprise AI inference workloads. More strikingly, 83% of enterprises now consider moving AI workloads back from public cloud — up from 69% in 2025 — and 50% have already done so. The gravity of data, sovereignty, and cost is pulling AI inference back toward private infrastructure faster than most architecture decisions have accounted for.

The Commodity Gap showed that the model layer is being commoditized faster than enterprise strategy has accounted for. The Energy Gap showed that AI infrastructure costs are creating a power crisis nobody budgeted for. The gravity gap is where both of those forces converge on a single architectural decision: where should inference actually run? The answer in 2026 is increasingly not public cloud — and the organizations that designed their agent architectures around public-cloud-first assumptions are facing the most expensive repatriation they never planned for.

The three mechanisms of the gravity gap

1. AI inference has a fundamentally different cost structure than traditional cloud compute — and most FinOps models don't account for it. Traditional cloud FinOps optimizes around compute, storage, and network. AI inference introduces a cost vector that behaves like none of them: data egress is the hidden tax that grows superlinearly with agent complexity. Every tool call an agent makes, every context window it retrieves, every multi-model verification it performs generates data movement that the GPU pricing model ignores. The Scaffolding Gap showed that infrastructure around agents costs 8x more than the models; the gravity gap is the geographical dimension of that same problem. A 4-agent workflow with MCP-based tool calling that runs 10,000 sessions per day generates approximately 1.7 TB of cross-service data transfer. At standard public cloud egress rates of $0.05-0.12/GB, that's $85-204 per day in bandwidth costs alone — before a single GPU minute is billed. Most enterprise FinOps dashboards track GPU utilization. Almost none track egress-to-inference ratio. The Unit Economics Gap showed that 88% of enterprises cannot calculate cost per completed workflow; the gravity gap is the specific reason why — your unit economics break the moment inference crosses a cloud boundary.

2. Sovereignty and compliance requirements make shared-responsibility models untenable for production AI. The Sovereignty Gap documented how the global AI market is fracturing into three incompatible stacks. The gravity gap is the infrastructure consequence: you cannot run EU AI Act-compliant inference on US-based GPU clusters without incurring data transfer costs that make the unit economics absurd. Broadcom's survey found that security and compliance rank as the single most important factor in workload placement for 32% of organizations, while data protection and privacy requirements drive placement for 37%. These are not soft preferences — they are hard architectural constraints. The Audit Gap showed that the average agent trace is 55x larger than traditional logs; the gravity gap means those traces cannot legally leave the jurisdiction where the data originated. Enterprises that designed for public cloud are discovering that compliance-driven repatriation is not a choice — it is a regulatory mandate they did not budget the 12-18 month migration cycle for.

3. The inference-to-training ratio flip rewrote the infrastructure cost equation. In 2024, enterprise AI spend was dominated by training: fine-tuning, R&D, model customization. In mid-2026, production inference accounts for ~65% of AI compute spend, per Deloitte's 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise report. Inference is fundamentally more sensitive to data gravity than training. Training is a batch process that can tolerate data movement, latency, and occasional egress spikes. Inference is a real-time, high-frequency operation where every millisecond of latency and every byte of cross-boundary data transfer compounds into a cost structure that scales linearly with adoption. The Jevons Trap showed that 67% cheaper tokens produce 320% higher bills; the gravity gap is the infrastructure corollary — cheaper inference drives more agents, more agents drive more data movement, and more data movement drives costs that the GPU-centric cost model never anticipated. Private cloud budgets are now growing at more than twice the pace of public cloud outlays for AI workloads, with net intent to grow private cloud spending jumping from 51% to 72% in a single year.

Four patterns that close the gravity gap

1. Data-gravity-aware architecture design. The first pattern is architectural: place inference where the data lives, not where GPUs are cheapest. This sounds obvious but violates every public-cloud-first assumption baked into the last decade of infrastructure design. For retrieval-augmented generation workloads, the vector database and the inference endpoint should be co-located — ideally in the same availability zone, same VPC, same Kubernetes cluster. The Retrieval Tax showed that vector database latency is the #1 scaling bottleneck; co-located inference reduces the cross-service data transfer that compounds that latency. For multi-agent workflows with MCP servers, each agent should prefer local MCP endpoints over remote ones, with cross-cluster communication reserved for inter-agent handoffs that cannot be colocated. This is not about avoiding public cloud entirely — it is about being intentional about where inference runs, and designing architectures that can move workloads without rewriting them.

2. FinOps models that track total inference cost, not GPU cost. The organizations that close the gravity gap measure a metric most FinOps dashboards do not: cost per inference, all-in. This includes GPU compute, data egress, inter-region bandwidth, NAT gateway charges, load balancer overhead, and the amortized cost of the compliance infrastructure required to run the workload in its jurisdiction. The FinOps Foundation's 2026 AI Cost Management Framework recommends treating egress-to-inference ratio (EBIR) as a first-class FinOps metric, with target ratios below 0.15:1 for production deployments. Organizations above 0.3:1 should trigger a repatriation review. Most enterprises that measure EBIR for the first time discover ratios of 0.8:1 to 2.5:1 — meaning they spend nearly as much or more on data movement as they do on actual inference. The metric alone closes the gap because it surfaces costs that have been invisible in every GPU-centric dashboard.

3. Sovereignty-first deployment planning as a default, not an exception. The teams that avoid the gravity gap do not retrofit compliance onto existing architectures — they plan for sovereignty constraints before the first inference runs. This means mapping regulatory requirements to infrastructure zones during the architecture design phase, not during the pre-launch compliance review. For EU-regulated workloads, inference runs on EU-based private cloud or sovereign cloud providers. For US-regulated healthcare data, inference stays on US-based infrastructure with HIPAA-compliant data boundaries. The pattern is simple: define the jurisdiction for every data class before you choose the infrastructure, then let the jurisdiction decision constrain the infrastructure choice. The Broadcom survey found that organizations practicing sovereignty-first deployment report 3.2x fewer repatriation surprises than those that provision infrastructure first and retrofit compliance later. The cost of planning for sovereignty upfront is nearly zero. The cost of retrofitting it after deployment is the most expensive infrastructure migration nobody budgeted for.

4. Build repatriation optionality into every agent architecture. The most expensive lesson in enterprise AI infrastructure is that the workload placement decision you make today will not be the right one in 18 months. Model providers will change their pricing. Regulations will shift. Your data gravity will increase. The organizations that win treat repatriation optionality as a design requirement: every agent should be deployable against a local or remote inference endpoint through a configuration change, not a code change. MCP-based architectures have an inherent advantage here — because the protocol standardizes the interface between agents and tools, swapping an inference backend is a configuration update rather than a rewrite. The Interoperability Gap showed that 76% of agent deployments lack standard communication protocols; the gravity gap is why that matters for infrastructure costs. Organizations using MCP-native architectures report 67% faster workload migration cycles than those using proprietary agent runtimes. Repatriation optionality is not about predicting where inference should run. It is about ensuring you can move it when the answer changes — because in enterprise AI in 2026, the answer always changes.

The gravity gap is the most expensive infrastructure blind spot in enterprise AI because it is caused by a success condition: your AI adoption is working, so you scale, and scaling across cloud boundaries compounds costs that the original architecture never accounted for. The 83% of enterprises now considering repatriation are not regressing — they are discovering that the public cloud AI architecture they inherited from the experimentation phase is structurally incompatible with the cost, compliance, and latency demands of production-scale inference. The four patterns — data-gravity-aware design, all-in FinOps, sovereignty-first planning, and repatriation optionality — are not about rejecting public cloud. They are about making workload placement an intentional decision rather than a default assumption. Because every inference request that crosses a cloud boundary you did not model is the most expensive infrastructure debt in enterprise AI — and it compounds with every agent you deploy.
