The Data Debt

June 16, 2026
AI Data Product Management

I watched a team spend six months building an agent that could handle customer refunds automatically. The model was great. The prompts were tight. The demo wowed the execs. Then they connected it to the actual data warehouse and it fell apart. Customer segments were nine months out of sync with the CRM. The product hierarchy had been reorganized twice since the training snapshot. The "churn probability" feature it relied on most was computed against a definition the team changed in January without telling anyone.

The model worked. The data was wrong.

This is data debt. And if you're deploying AI in an enterprise, you're carrying it whether you know it or not.

Genpact and HFS Research just published a study of 2,000 enterprise executives that puts some numbers on this. They found roughly $18 trillion in trapped enterprise value — value that's locked up because legacy data practices can't support modern AI workloads. Only 33% of enterprise data is AI-ready. Two-thirds of the data feeding your AI systems is effectively a liability, and it compounds every quarter you don't address it.

I think about this as a product manager. If your model is a Ferrari and your data is a dirt road, the Ferrari doesn't go faster. It just overheats. We spend so much time optimizing models and so little time asking whether the data underneath them is any good.

The death spiral

Here's the pattern I keep seeing. A team deploys an agent on a small set of clean data. Works great — 94% task completion, glowing feedback. The exec sponsor says scale it up. But scaling means connecting to the enterprise data warehouse, the CRM, the ERP, the legacy mainframe. Each new source introduces latency, schema drift, missing fields. Performance drops to 67%. The team blames the model. The model is fine. The data foundation never scaled with the ambition.

Bain's 2026 Automation and AI Pathfinder Survey backs this up. The companies that actually hit their AI targets cite data as a bigger barrier than the ones that missed. They haven't solved the data problem — they've just run into it harder because they're deploying at scale.

What separates the 6%

The Genpact study found that only 6% of companies have actually resolved these debts at scale. The difference isn't better models. It's that they treat data as a first-class budget item, not an afterthought.

Gartner found that organizations with successful AI outcomes invest up to four times more in data quality and governance as a percentage of revenue. Four times. Not because they're wasting money — because they know that every dollar of AI spend needs a corresponding dollar of data foundation investment.

The most successful teams I've seen do three things the rest don't. First, they have a funded data debt resolution program with executive ownership — not a "data quality working group that meets quarterly." Second, they measure data readiness the same way they measure model performance. They can tell you the freshness, completeness, and consistency of the data feeding their model, not just the model's accuracy. Third, they fix broken processes before deploying AI. AI doesn't fix process debt — it locks it in and speeds it up.

Data debt sits underneath every other AI problem. You can have the best governance, the best observability, the best enforcement controls — but if your data is stale, inconsistent, or incomplete, every layer above it is built on sand.
