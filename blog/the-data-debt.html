The Data Debt: Why 85% of Leaders Say Data Gaps Are Killing Their AI Value

June 16, 2026
AI Data Product Management

Your agent just approved a strategic pricing change. The recommendation was precise, well-reasoned, and accompanied by a detailed confidence interval. There was only one problem: the customer segment data feeding the model was nine months out of sync with the CRM, the product hierarchy had been reorganized twice since the training snapshot, and the "churn probability" feature it weighted most heavily was computed against a definition your team changed in January. The agent performed flawlessly. The data was wrong.

This is the hidden tax every enterprise AI initiative pays before it generates a dollar of value. Genpact and HFS Research's global study of 2,000 enterprise executives, published June 15, 2026, identifies nearly $18 trillion in recoverable enterprise value trapped inside Global 2000 companies — and names four interconnected debts preventing organizations from unlocking it: data, process, technology, and talent. 85% of leaders surveyed say these debts are actively limiting their AI value, and over half have no funded plan to address them. With nearly 13% of average function spend now flowing into AI, the gap between ambition and foundation has never been more expensive.

Three numbers explain why data debt has become the single largest drag on enterprise AI returns — and why most organizations don't even know they're carrying it.

$18 trillion. That's the recoverable enterprise value Genpact and HFS Research mapped across Global 2000 companies — trapped because legacy data practices can't support modern AI workloads. The finding cuts against the dominant narrative that AI value depends primarily on model capability. If the model is a Ferrari and your data is a dirt road, the Ferrari doesn't go faster. It just overheats. The report shows that only 33% of enterprise data is AI-ready today. Two-thirds of the data feeding your AI systems is effectively liability, not asset — and it compounds every quarter the data foundation goes unaddressed.

42%. The share of AI and analytics initiatives already failing because of data quality issues, according to the same Genpact study. This is not a future risk — it is the current baseline. Gartner has been projecting since 2024 that 60% of AI projects unsupported by AI-ready data will be abandoned through 2026. Bain & Company's 2026 Automation and AI Pathfinder Survey of 951 global companies found that data access and integration is the #1 barrier to AI progress, cited by 41% of respondents — ahead of compliance concerns, budget constraints, skills gaps, and executive buy-in. The data problem is not hypothetical. It is the dominant failure mode of enterprise AI, and it is getting worse because deployments scale faster than data foundations improve.

4x. The multiplier Gartner found in organizations that report successful AI outcomes: they invest up to four times more (as a percentage of revenue) in data quality, governance, AI-ready people, and change management compared to organizations reporting poor AI outcomes. Cognizant's June 2026 research, Closing the AI Execution Gap, corroborates this: organizations with strong data foundations report nearly 27% higher productivity gains and are 20%+ less likely to abandon AI initiatives. They also found that 64.5% of organizations have at least one of five key data dimensions rated adequate or below. The difference between success and failure is not a better model — it is a better data foundation.

The four debts that compound into one

The Genpact framework breaks enterprise debt into four categories, and data debt is the one that amplifies all the others. Data debt is the gap between the data you have and the data AI needs. Process debt is the cost of how work actually flows — manual, ungoverned, and hard to change (around 40% of employee time is lost to inefficient processes). Technology debt is the legacy infrastructure tax — core enterprise systems average 10 years old, and 42% of developer time goes to servicing existing debt rather than building new capabilities. Talent debt is the readiness gap — only 32% of the workforce is AI-ready.

Data debt is the most consequential because it makes every other debt harder to resolve. Bad data hides inside broken processes, making them invisible to redesign. Bad data calcifies on legacy systems, making migration riskier. Bad data undermines AI tools that could otherwise augment an undertrained workforce, making talent investment feel pointless. The Genpact data is blunt: 85% of leaders say these four debts are limiting their AI value. But only 6% of respondents — identified as "proven debt resolvers" — have established, run, and measured results from resolution programs at scale.

The data debt death spiral

Here is the pattern that should keep every AI PM up at night. An organization deploys an AI agent on a subset of clean, curated data. The agent performs well in pilot — 94% task completion, glowing user feedback. The executive sponsor approves scale-up. But scaling means connecting to the enterprise data warehouse, the CRM, the ERP, and the legacy mainframe. Each new data source introduces latency, schema drift, missing fields, and definition mismatches. Performance drops from 94% to 67%. The team blames the model. The model is fine. The data foundation never scaled with the ambition.

The Bain data surfaces this asymmetry directly: the companies that hit their AI targets cite data as a bigger barrier than those that missed — 44% compared with 40%. They have not solved the data problem. They have run into it harder because they are deploying at scale. The underperformers cite organizational obstacles instead: insufficient budget, lack of a center of excellence, competing priorities. Those are not data problems. They are signals that AI has never been given the mandate it requires. The data debt spiral claims both camps — but one group discovers it early because they shipped something real.

What the 6% do differently

The Genpact research identifies what separates the 6% of proven debt resolvers from the 94% who are stuck. The gap between the groups on diagnosis is small. The gap on execution is enormous.

They treat data debt as a board-level problem, not an IT problem. The data foundation decisions that determine AI outcomes — schema governance, master data management, data quality SLAs — are not technical choices. They are structural business decisions about how the organization defines and represents its own reality. The 6% have a funded, staffed, and measured data debt resolution program with executive ownership. The other 94% have a data quality working group that meets quarterly.

They fund data infrastructure as a percentage of AI budget from day one. The 4x Gartner multiplier is not an accident — it is a deliberate allocation choice. These organizations assume that every dollar of AI spend requires a corresponding dollar of data foundation investment. They do not fund AI and "figure out data later." They treat data readiness as a gating prerequisite, not a post-hoc optimization.

They measure data readiness the same way they measure model performance. Most AI teams can tell you their model's accuracy, latency, and cost per inference. Almost none can tell you the freshness, completeness, and consistency of the data feeding that model. The 6% have data quality dashboards that sit alongside model monitoring dashboards. They know when a source schema drifts before the agent produces a wrong answer. They have data SLAs the same way they have inference SLAs.

They resolve process debt before deploying AI. The single most costly mistake in enterprise AI — reinforced across every study this year — is automating a broken process. AI doesn't fix process debt; it locks it in and speeds it up. The 6% invest in process redesign as a prerequisite to AI deployment. They ask "what would this workflow look like if we designed it today?" before they ask "where can we add AI?"

The arc, continued

This series has traced the real barriers to enterprise AI — the unglamorous infrastructure problems that separate demos from production. The Great Cheapening made experimentation free. The 88% Problem showed that pilots stall at governance. The Governance Multiplier proved that guardrails ship more. The 68-Point Gap revealed the distance between claiming and depending. The Enforcement Gap showed policies without runtime controls. The Mandate Trap argued you can't review your way to maturity. The Observability Blindspot showed why you need to see what your agents are doing. The Deprecation Crisis surfaced the hidden cost of model version churn. The Value Chasm exposed the gap between AI spend and AI return. The Fluency Gap showed that technology without literacy is infrastructure without users.

Data debt sits underneath all of them. You can have the best governance framework, the most observability-rich agent platform, the tightest enforcement controls, and the most AI-literate workforce in the world — but if the data feeding your agents is stale, inconsistent, or incomplete, every layer above it operates on a foundation of sand. The companies that break out of the 6% ceiling are not the ones with the best models. They are the ones that finally treated data as a first-class line item in the AI budget.
