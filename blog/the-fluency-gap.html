The Fluency Gap

June 16, 2026
AI Workforce Product Management

Your company just bought 5,000 Copilot seats. The CEO announced an "AI-first" transformation at the all-hands. One month later, adoption is at 12% and nobody can explain why a tool that looked magical in the demo is gathering dust on a thousand taskbars.

I've seen this play out at more organizations than I can count. It's not a technology problem. It's a literacy problem. And it's the biggest hidden tax on enterprise AI today.

The data backs this up. Writer's 2026 survey of 2,400 employees found that 79% of organizations face significant challenges translating AI adoption into measurable business value — despite higher budgets than ever. Enterprise AI spending hit $148 billion in Q1 2026 alone, up 47% year over year. The budgets are there. The literacy isn't.

McKinsey's 2026 State of AI survey found the same thing from a different angle. 67% of executives cite organizational resistance as the single biggest barrier to capturing AI value. Not model accuracy. Not data quality. Not integration complexity. Resistance. People — smart, accomplished, well-intentioned people — are choosing not to use the tools their companies are spending fortunes on.

And honestly? Their resistance makes sense.

Why nobody wants to use your AI tool

I've sat through enough adoption post-mortems to know that when employees resist AI, they usually have good reasons. The tool doesn't integrate with their actual workflow. It hallucinates on critical data and there's no way to flag errors. It takes more effort to fix the output than to just do the work from scratch. And the threat to their role is unspoken but real.

These aren't Luddite objections. They're rational risk assessments that organizations haven't addressed because they're focused on procuring the technology, not building the trust infrastructure around it.

Most companies respond by buying training. A half-day workshop on prompt engineering. A Coursera course. Call it a day. Deloitte's 2026 State of AI report confirms this — education was the #1 talent strategy adjustment, but only 12% of organizations have redesigned their operating model at scale to actually accommodate AI. The gap between "we know everything is changing" and "we've actually changed anything" is the fluency gap in its purest form.

There's a framework making the rounds called the 10/20/70 model: 10% of effort on algorithms, 20% on tech and data infrastructure, 70% on people, processes, and organizational change. Most organizations flip this. They spend 70% on licensing and 10% on the human infrastructure. The allocation itself is the problem.

What fluency actually looks like

Real AI fluency isn't about writing perfect prompts. That's table stakes and it's a rapidly depreciating skill anyway. Real fluency is knowing what not to apply AI to. Every organization has a graveyard of projects that solved a non-existent problem with an LLM because a team wanted to use AI more than they wanted to solve the problem.

It's also understanding the cost profile of AI decisions. A simple classification call costs fractions of a cent. A multi-step agentic reasoning chain can cost dollars. The organizations capturing value are the ones where PMs and engineers can estimate inference costs the same way they estimate compute costs. That's still rare.

And it's designing for the failure case first. Most agent implementations spend 70% of effort getting the model to perform perfectly and 10% figuring out what happens when it doesn't. That's backwards. Fluency means designing the feedback loop — a mechanism for users to flag bad outputs, for the system to learn from corrections, for the governance framework to adapt as usage patterns emerge.

The technology is ready. The budgets are approved. The question that will separate the small minority of companies that actually get value from the rest is whether they're willing to invest in fluency with the same seriousness they invest in infrastructure.
