The Redesign Gap: Why 66% of Enterprises Are Using AI Without Changing How Work Gets Done

June 23, 2026
AI Product Management Workforce Strategy

Your company just deployed an AI agent that handles customer refunds. It processes requests in seconds instead of days. The metrics look great — response time down 80%, throughput up 5x. But the refund policy hasn't changed. The approval chain hasn't changed. The way exceptions are escalated hasn't changed. The agent just made an unchanged process 5x faster at doing something that maybe shouldn't have been done 5x faster.

This is 2026's most expensive invisible cost in enterprise AI: the redesign gap. Organizations are deploying AI at record velocity into workflows that were designed for humans, paper, and batch processing. The AI makes the old process faster. It does not make it better.

Deloitte's 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise report surveyed 2,800+ leaders across 15+ countries and found that only 34% of organizations are truly reimagining their businesses around AI — creating new products, reinventing core processes, or building new business models. Another 30% are redesigning key processes. The remaining 37% are using AI at a surface level with little or no change to existing processes.

The math is brutal: nearly two-thirds of AI investment is flowing through unchanged operating models. The McKinsey Global Institute found that only 6% of organizations qualify as "AI high performers" — the ones seeing material EBIT impact from AI. The other 94% are generating activity, not value. The correlation between those two numbers is not a coincidence.

This is the hidden reason behind the value chasm I traced last week — 90% of companies doubling AI budgets while returns stay flat. When you layer AI on existing processes without redesigning those processes, you capture efficiency gains at best. You do not capture transformation. And efficiency gains on a broken process are just faster brokenness.

The three traps keeping enterprises from redesigning

Across the Deloitte data and my own conversations with product leaders, three structural patterns keep organizations in the optimization trap:

1. The Education Reflex. When asked how they're adjusting talent strategy for AI, 53% of organizations said educating the broader workforce to raise AI fluency — the most common response by a wide margin. Only 19% said re-architecting roles and workflows. The instinct is to teach people how to use the new tool. The harder work — redesigning the job around the tool — is deferred.

The result is what I called the fluency gap in an earlier post: organizations have the budget but not the literacy. But even literacy isn't enough when the workflows themselves aren't redesigned. You can train every employee on prompt engineering, and they'll still be submitting expense reports through a six-approval chain designed in 2018.

2. The ROI Measurement Trap. Most organizations measure AI success through efficiency metrics — cost per transaction, response time, throughput. These metrics are easy to measure and easy to improve. But they actively discourage redesign. If your bonus is tied to "reduce customer support cost per ticket by 20%," you will optimize the existing ticket workflow with AI. You will not ask whether the ticket workflow should exist at all.

Deloitte found that twice as many leaders as last year report transformative impact from AI — but the absolute number is still only 34%. The organizations breaking through are the ones measuring AI impact against business outcomes (revenue per customer, time to market, NPS) rather than operational efficiency alone. The ones stuck in the optimization trap are measuring against cost-per-something and wondering why the math doesn't compound.

3. The Governance Shortcut. The governance multiplier showed that companies with AI governance ship 12x more projects to production. But governance without redesign is governance theater — you've approved the agent to operate within the existing workflow, without asking if the existing workflow is the right target.

The enforcement gap showed that 82% of execs trust their AI policies but only 14% can enforce them. The redesign gap is the same pattern at the process layer: organizations are approving AI deployments against process models that haven't been updated since before the AI existed.

What the reimaginers do differently

The 34% who are redesigning share three patterns that the 66% haven't adopted. These are not technology choices. They are organizational design choices.

1. They start with the question "should this process exist?" Before deploying AI into an existing workflow, the reimaginers ask: if we were designing this process from scratch today, knowing what AI can do, would we design it this way? The answer is almost always no. The existing process was shaped by human constraints — batch processing, approval chains, handoffs between departments — that AI eliminates. The reimaginers design around AI capabilities, not around legacy org charts.

2. They create new roles, not just new tools. Deloitte found that the most successful organizations are creating roles that didn't exist two years ago: AI operations managers, human-AI interaction specialists, quality stewards. These roles signal a structural shift: AI is not a tool bolted onto existing jobs. It is a new component of how work is organized. The organizations still treating AI as "my existing job, now with an assistant" are optimizing. The ones creating new roles are redesigning.

This mirrors the mandate trap argument: performance reviews can't force AI adoption because they operate within unchanged job definitions. You can't review your way to maturity. You redesign your way there.

3. They measure outcomes, not throughput. The reimaginers define success as "did the customer get the right answer faster and with less friction?" rather than "how many tickets did we process per agent per hour?" The distinction matters because the first question invites process redesign — maybe the customer shouldn't need to submit a ticket at all. The second question optimizes the ticket system AI should be replacing.

The evaluation gap showed that 64% of leaders cite evaluation gaps as the top blocker for AI production. But evaluation frameworks that measure throughput metrics are evaluating the wrong thing. They tell you how efficiently the old process runs. They don't tell you whether the process is the right one.

The redesign gap closes at the org chart level

The observability blindspot showed that 80% of enterprises can't debug their AI agents. The coordination gap showed that only 12% coordinate multi-agent systems well. The redesign gap is the organizational prerequisite for both: you cannot debug what you haven't redesigned, and you cannot coordinate workflows that were never designed to work together in the first place.

The 66% figure from Deloitte — two-thirds of organizations using AI without redesigning processes — is the meta-gap underlying every technical gap I've written about in this series. It explains why the 88% pilot failure rate persists despite better models. It explains why the data debt keeps accumulating. It explains why the value chasm is widening even as investment grows.

McKinsey's 6% AI high-performer number is the most important metric in enterprise AI right now, because it tells us that organizational design — not model quality, not infrastructure, not even governance — is the binding constraint. The organizations closing the redesign gap are not necessarily the ones with the best AI. They are the ones willing to ask the uncomfortable question: what would this process look like if AI had never existed, and we were designing it from scratch?

Most organizations won't ask that question because the answer threatens existing structures, headcount models, and power dynamics. The ones that do ask will be the 6% who see real returns. The rest will keep optimizing processes that should have been retired, wondering why their AI investment doesn't compound.
