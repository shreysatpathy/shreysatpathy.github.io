The Redesign Gap: Why 66% of Enterprises Are Using AI Without Changing How Work Gets Done

June 23, 2026
AI Product Management Workforce Strategy

I've been watching enterprises pour money into AI for a while now. And I keep seeing the same pattern: they bolt AI onto processes that were designed for a world without AI. The process stays the same. It's just faster now.

Your company deploys an AI agent that handles customer refunds. Response time drops 80%. Throughput shoots up. Everyone high-fives. But the refund policy hasn't changed. The approval chain hasn't changed. The way exceptions get escalated hasn't changed. You just made an unchanged process 5x faster at doing something that maybe shouldn't be done 5x faster.

I call this the redesign gap. It's 2026's most expensive invisible cost in enterprise AI.

Deloitte's latest State of AI in the Enterprise report surveyed 2,800+ leaders. Only 34% are truly reimagining their businesses around AI — creating new products, reinventing core processes. Another 30% are redesigning key processes. The remaining 37% are using AI at a surface level with little to no change to existing processes.

That means nearly two-thirds of AI investment is flowing through unchanged operating models. McKinsey found that only 6% of organizations qualify as "AI high performers" — the ones seeing material impact from AI. The other 94% are generating activity, not value. That's not a coincidence.

The three traps keeping enterprises stuck

From the Deloitte data and my own conversations with product leaders, I see three patterns keeping organizations in the optimization trap.

1. The Education Reflex. When asked how they're adjusting talent strategy for AI, 53% of organizations said educating the workforce on AI fluency. Only 19% said re-architecting roles and workflows. The instinct is to teach people how to use the new tool. The harder work — redesigning the job around the tool — gets deferred. You can train every employee on prompt engineering, and they'll still be submitting expense reports through a six-approval chain designed in 2018.

2. The ROI Measurement Trap. Most organizations measure AI success through efficiency metrics — cost per transaction, response time, throughput. These are easy to measure and easy to improve. But they actively discourage redesign. If your bonus is tied to "reduce customer support cost per ticket by 20%," you'll optimize the existing ticket workflow with AI. You won't ask whether the ticket workflow should exist at all.

The organizations breaking through measure AI impact against business outcomes — revenue per customer, time to market, NPS — rather than operational efficiency alone. The ones stuck in the trap measure against cost-per-something and wonder why the math doesn't compound.

3. The Governance Shortcut. You can have the best AI governance in the world, but if you're governing a process that shouldn't exist, you're just doing theater. Organizations approve AI agents to operate within existing workflows without asking if those workflows are the right target.

What the reimaginers do differently

The 34% who are redesigning share three patterns. These aren't technology choices. They're organizational design choices.

1. They start with "should this process exist?" Before deploying AI into an existing workflow, they ask: if we were designing this process from scratch today, knowing what AI can do, would we design it this way? The answer is almost always no. Existing processes were shaped by human constraints — batch processing, approval chains, handoffs between departments — that AI eliminates.

2. They create new roles, not just new tools. The most successful organizations are creating roles that didn't exist two years ago: AI operations managers, human-AI interaction specialists, quality stewards. These roles signal a structural shift. AI is not a tool bolted onto existing jobs. It's a new component of how work is organized.

3. They measure outcomes, not throughput. Success is "did the customer get the right answer faster and with less friction?" not "how many tickets did we process per hour?" The first question invites process redesign — maybe the customer shouldn't need to submit a ticket at all. The second optimizes the system AI should be replacing.

The redesign gap closes at the org chart level

McKinsey's 6% number is the most important metric in enterprise AI right now. It tells us that organizational design — not model quality, not infrastructure, not governance — is the binding constraint. The organizations closing the redesign gap aren't necessarily the ones with the best AI. They're the ones willing to ask the uncomfortable question: what would this process look like if AI had never existed, and we were designing it from scratch?

Most organizations won't ask that question because the answer threatens existing structures, headcount models, and power dynamics. The ones that do ask will be the 6% who see real returns. The rest will keep optimizing processes that should have been retired.
