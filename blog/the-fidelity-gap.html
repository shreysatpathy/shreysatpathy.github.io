The Fidelity Gap: Why Information Degrades 35% Per Hop in Multi-Agent Chains — And Why Every Agent-to-Agent Handoff Is the Most Expensive Signal Loss Nobody Instrumented For

July 21, 2026
AI Infrastructure Architecture Product Management

Your customer service agent resolved a billing issue and handed off to the refund agent with a summary: "Customer overcharged by $47.23 on invoice INV-8921 due to proration error. Authorization confirmed. Priority: high." The refund agent received: "Customer overcharged ~$47 on an invoice, proration issue, proceed with refund." The audit agent received: "Refund case, ~$50, standard processing." By the time the case reached the escalation agent, the note read: "Customer dispute, amount unclear, check history." Three handoffs. Four agents. Zero fidelity. The information degradation wasn't a bug — it's the natural behavior of LLM-based agents compressing context at each hop. And 57% of enterprises now run multi-agent systems in production without measuring a single byte of what gets lost between them.

This is the fidelity gap, and I think it's the most expensive invisible architecture debt in enterprise AI right now. Unlike the Fragility Tax, which is about systems failing more as they scale, the fidelity gap is about systems that appear to succeed while silently degrading the signal they're supposed to transmit. Unlike the Context Gap, which is about different agents lacking shared context, the fidelity gap is about active context destruction during handoffs — agents that have context but compress, infer, and hallucinate when passing it along. And unlike the Handoff Gap, which is about agents delivering to humans, the fidelity gap is about agents delivering to other agents and the telephone game that follows.

A June 2026 study from the Agentic AI Institute found that 72% of enterprises now have agentic AI in production, but only 12% have any form of cross-agent fidelity monitoring. The 60-point gap between deployment and measurement is not a monitoring problem. It's an architecture problem. Teams build agent chains assuming that each agent passes information faithfully — an assumption that every LLM-based system violates by design. The model doesn't transmit information. It reconstructs it from a compressed representation, and every reconstruction introduces noise.

The three mechanisms of fidelity loss

Fidelity loss in multi-agent chains isn't a single failure mode. It's three distinct mechanisms that compound across handoffs. Understanding each one is the prerequisite to instrumenting for it.

1. Summarization compression. When Agent A passes context to Agent B, it never passes the raw conversation. It summarizes. The summarization is lossy by design — LLMs trade detail for length, and the trade function is invisible to the engineer. An internal analysis by a major ticketing platform, shared at the ICLR 2026 Agentic AI workshop in April, found that single-hop summarization preserved only 64% of task-critical entities on average. After two hops, that dropped to 41%. After three hops, 22%. The platform had been running a 4-agent escalation chain for 18 months without ever measuring this. The degradation was invisible because each individual agent's output looked coherent — it just contained progressively less of the information that mattered.

2. Inferential gap-filling. When Agent B receives an incomplete summary from Agent A, it doesn't flag the gap. It fills it. LLMs are next-token predictors optimized for plausible completions, not faithful transmission. An agent that receives "customer contacted about order issue, resolution pending" will infer a dozen details — order type, issue category, customer sentiment — none of which came from Agent A. This is not a hallucination in the classic sense because the agent isn't generating false facts about the external world. It's generating false facts about what Agent A actually said. The Nature paper published June 10, 2026 on hallucination detection in LLMs documented this exact failure mode: models have calibration error rates above 70% when asked to assess the accuracy of their own inferences. Agents that fill in missing context don't know they're doing it, and they certainly can't tell you which parts of their output are transmitted versus invented.

3. Context window attrition. The most banal mechanism is also the most destructive. Every agent in a chain has a context window limit. When the accumulated context from all previous agents exceeds that limit, the agent must choose what to keep and what to drop. The choice is governed by the model's internal attention mechanism, not by any semantic importance signal. Critical details — dollar amounts, timestamps, customer IDs, decision rationales — are evicted with the same probability as filler text. The Retrieval Tax showed that context window memory costs are the #1 AI scaling bottleneck. The Fidelity Gap is the consequence: when context windows overflow, fidelity doesn't degrade gracefully. It falls off a cliff.

The convergence that nobody measures

These three mechanisms don't operate independently. They compound. Summarization creates the conditions for inferential gap-filling, which consumes context window budget, which forces further summarization. Each agent in the chain accelerates the degradation that the next agent will experience.

The KPMG Q1 2026 AI Pulse Survey found that 54% of organizations are actively deploying AI agents across core operations, up from 11% two years ago. The Gartner prediction that 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by end of 2026 means the average agent chain is about to get longer. More agents means more handoffs. More handoffs means more fidelity loss. The Scaffolding Gap showed that the infrastructure around agents costs 8x more than the models themselves. But that infrastructure — evaluation frameworks, guardrails, observability pipelines — is almost universally built to measure individual agent performance, not cross-agent signal fidelity. Teams can tell you the accuracy of Agent A and the latency of Agent B. They cannot tell you what information Agent A transmitted to Agent B that Agent B actually acted on.

This connects to the Fragility Tax in a way the industry hasn't fully connected. The Fragility Tax showed that multi-agent systems fail 10x more at 5x scale. The Fidelity Gap explains one of the primary mechanisms: as you add agents, the information that each agent acts on is increasingly degraded, so each agent makes worse decisions, which creates more recovery work for downstream agents, which compounds the fidelity loss further. Fragility is the symptom. Fidelity loss is the cause.

The Google Cloud Next 2026 session on scaling AI agents in production, held April 23, covered production observability, sandboxed execution, and cold start optimization. It did not cover cross-agent fidelity measurement. The ICLR 2026 workshop on Agentic AI in the Wild, held April 27, had sessions on hallucination detection, multi-agent reinforcement learning collapse, and epistemic context learning. It did not have a session on information fidelity across agent handoffs. The industry is building the infrastructure for individual agent reliability without building the infrastructure for inter-agent signal integrity. The fidelity gap exists because nobody thought to measure what gets lost in translation between agent A and agent B — because everyone assumed that agents, unlike humans, transmit information faithfully.

Four patterns that close the fidelity gap

Across the organizations that are starting to measure cross-agent fidelity — and there are very few — four consistent patterns emerge. None of them require new models. All of them require changing how you think about agent architecture.

1. Structured handoff contracts. Instead of passing natural language summaries between agents, define typed handoff schemas that specify exactly which fields must be populated, which are optional, and which must be verified. A handoff contract for a customer escalation chain might specify: customer_id: string (required), issue_category: enum (required), dollar_amount: float (optional, verified), resolution_status: enum (required), agent_reasoning: string (optional, unverified). When Agent A hands off to Agent B, the runtime validates the contract before Agent B processes it. Missing required fields trigger a refusal, not an inference. This is the agentic equivalent of typed API contracts — something the software engineering world solved decades ago but the AI agent world is only now discovering it needs.

2. Per-hop fidelity telemetry. Just as you instrument LLM calls for latency, token count, and response quality, instrument the handoff itself. Before Agent B processes Agent A's output, snapshot a vector embedding of the transmitted context. After Agent B completes its step, compare the embedding similarity between what it received and what it acted on. A fidelity score below a threshold triggers an alert and a human review. The June 2026 Nature paper on semantic entropy provides a reusable methodology: measure the agreement between transmitted and reconstructed information at the semantic level, not the token level. Teams that implement per-hop telemetry report catching fidelity loss patterns — "Agent C consistently drops dollar amounts above $1,000" — that were invisible in aggregate success metrics.

3. Immutable shared context stores. The most effective antidote to fidelity loss is to stop passing context through agents entirely. Instead, have every agent read from and write to an immutable shared context store. Agent A writes its findings. Agent B reads them directly. No summarization, no compression, no inferential gap-filling. The shared store becomes the source of truth, and each agent's contribution is independently auditable. This pattern already exists in the event-sourcing and CQRS literature — architectures that separate write models from read models to prevent data degradation in distributed systems. The Memory Bank and Sessions services announced at Google Cloud Next 2026, as part of their revamped agent runtime, are early commercial implementations of this pattern. The teams that adopt shared context stores report near-zero fidelity loss across handoffs, at the cost of increased storage and retrieval overhead — a trade the Retrieval Tax makes explicit.

4. Agent handoff testing as a standard practice. Every agent integration test today tests the agent's ability to complete a task. Almost none test the agent's ability to receive context from another agent and preserve its informational content. The teams ahead of the fidelity curve run handoff-specific test suites: inject a known payload into Agent A, capture what Agent B receives after the handoff, and assert that critical information is preserved. This is the agentic equivalent of contract testing in microservice architectures — and it's just as essential. The McKinsey 2026 survey found that only 31% of enterprises have at least one AI agent in production across banking, insurance, and tech — the sectors that are furthest ahead — and even those teams rarely test handoff fidelity. The standard should be that no multi-agent system deploys without proving that information survives each handoff boundary.

The fidelity threshold

Here's the uncomfortable math: if each agent handoff preserves 65% of critical information (the median from the ticketing platform analysis), a 3-agent chain preserves 27%. A 5-agent chain preserves 12%. Most enterprises running multi-agent systems today are operating on 12-27% of the information they think they're operating on. They are making production decisions — refunding customers, escalating cases, modifying accounts — based on a signal that has been degraded by 73-88% from its source. The agents appear to work. The workflows complete. The success metrics look fine. But the information that drove those decisions was mostly reconstructed, inferred, and invented by intermediate agents that were never designed to be faithful transmitters.

The teams that treat cross-agent fidelity as a first-class architectural property — structured handoff contracts, per-hop telemetry, shared context stores, handoff testing — will be the ones whose multi-agent systems actually scale. The teams that don't will discover that the most expensive signal loss in enterprise AI isn't the model hallucinating about the world. It's your best agent hallucinating about what your other agent said.
