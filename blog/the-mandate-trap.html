The Mandate Trap: Why Performance Reviews Can't Force AI Adoption

June 16, 2026
AI Workforce Product Management

Meta made "AI-driven impact" a core performance criterion for every employee in January. Google managers are telling staff that AI usage will factor into reviews. The logic sounds right: measure what matters, reward it, adoption follows.

I don't think it's that simple.

Here's what keeps bothering me. Nearly half of organizations introduced AI without redesigning workflows or roles. Not a failure to adopt — a failure to adapt. The tool arrived, the work stayed the same, and the value never showed up. Meanwhile, a huge chunk of companies that adopted an AI tool in 2024 had already replaced it by early 2026. And only one in five AI investments delivers measurable ROI.

The mandate trend isn't malicious. It's premature. We're forcing adoption at the performance-review level while the foundations — workflow redesign, integration, measurement — haven't been built.

What actually works

The companies closing the adoption gap share a pattern that looks nothing like a mandate.

They redesign work before deploying AI. They don't bolt AI onto existing processes. They map the workflow, find the bottlenecks, and then ask: where does AI actually help? The answer is almost never "everywhere."

They invest in integration, not just licenses. AI tools that connect to your data, your compliance layer, and your existing APIs survive. Standalone tools — no matter how good the demo — get replaced within 18 months. A mandate from the C-suite doesn't change the integration math.

They measure outcomes, not activity. The high-performers don't ask "how many employees used Copilot?" They ask "how much faster do we resolve tickets?" and "what percentage of PRs bypass rework?" Input metrics reward compliance. Output metrics reward transformation.

What the mandate trend gets right

AI usage should be a core part of every knowledge worker's toolkit in 2026. Cheap inference made it economically irrational not to use it. The question is how you get there.

Mandates from above produce compliance behavior. Employees use AI to satisfy the review criteria, not to transform how they work. The difference between the two is the difference between activity and dependence.

The companies that win the AI adoption race aren't the ones with the toughest requirements. They're the ones that redesign work, integrate deeply, measure outcomes, and let adoption follow from value rather than compliance.

Meta and Google are asking the right question — how do we get everyone using AI effectively? They're just using the wrong lever.
