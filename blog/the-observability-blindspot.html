The Observability Blindspot: Why 80% of Enterprises Can't Debug Their AI Agents

June 16, 2026
AI Observability Product Management

I was on a call last week with a team that had an agent approve a $200K refund to the wrong customer. The trace said "reasoning completed successfully." The logs were clean. The agent, as far as it could tell, did exactly what it was told. They had no idea why it made that decision — and no tooling to find out.

This is happening everywhere right now. Everyone's so focused on getting agents to work at all that nobody's asked the obvious question: once they're in production, how do you debug them?

I've been banging this drum for a while, but I think observability is the next real bottleneck. Not model quality. Not even governance. Day 2 operations for agentic systems. Nobody has good tooling for it yet.

Three things current tooling doesn't handle

1. Tracing breaks the second an agent starts thinking. Normal distributed tracing assumes the request path is predictable. An agent's reasoning chain is anything but — it explores branches, backtracks, calls tools in random order, loops. When your agent spends 47 seconds "thinking" before deleting a production row, most APM tools show you one span labeled "llm.reason" with zero detail. That's not observability. That's a black box with a timestamp.

2. Nobody knows where the money goes. An agentic workflow might call the model 15 times — some for reasoning, some for tool selection, some for generation. Which step drove the cost? Which customer triggered the expensive chain? Most teams can tell you their monthly OpenAI total. Almost none can tell you which agent or workflow produced it. Finance will eventually force this, and it won't be pretty.

3. Failures are silent. A regular service throws 500s. An agent "succeeds" at calling the wrong tool. It "completes" a task with garbage output. Without observability, every deployment is a leap of faith. Most organizations choose not to leap — and that's why so many pilots die before reaching production.

What the teams running agents in production do differently

A small minority of teams are actually running multi-agent systems reliably. Here's what they've invested in:

Reasoning traces. Not just "what the LLM returned," but the full decision tree. Which prompt was selected. Which tools were considered and rejected. Why the final action was chosen. It's closer to a debugger than a logger. That's the difference between "we see the error" and "we understand the mistake."

Cost budgets per step. Each decision step gets an inference budget. If an agent exceeds 3 model calls or 500ms per reasoning step, it surfaces mid-workflow — not after. Cost becomes a per-transaction metric instead of a monthly surprise.

Explicit success criteria. "Did the agent finish" is the wrong question. The right question is "did the output satisfy the criteria we defined before it ran?" Teams that define machine-verifiable success criteria per workflow can classify failures without human review.

You can have the best governance policies in the world. But if you can't see what your agents are doing, you're flying blind. And nobody budgets for a blind fleet.
